## Cell counting with hemocytometer

In the Barott lab, we primarily measure symbiont density (or cell counts) on the Guava (protocol [here](https://github.com/JillAshey/Ashey_Barott_Lab_Notebook/blob/main/posts/2026-06-11-Guava.md)). However, we can also manually count cells using a hemocytometer. This can be done as a check to see if the Guava cell counts make sense or if the Guava is broken/down when you are airbrushing.

A hemocytometer is a special microscope slide used to manually count cells in a suspension. There are two counting grids on the slide. 

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Hemocytometer_with_gloved_hand.JPG/500px-Hemocytometer_with_gloved_hand.JPG)

When put under the microscope with no sample, the hemocytometer looks like this: 

![](https://github.com/urol-e5/protocols/blob/master/images/hema1.png?raw=true)

![](https://github.com/urol-e5/protocols/blob/master/images/hema2.png?raw=true)

The grid can be used to count cells. 

### Materials 

- hemocytometer w/ cover slip
- Light microscope 
- Glass pipette (or p20)
- DI water 
- Kim wipes 

### Protocol

- Thaw symbiont samples on ice. 
- Put the hemocytometer and cover slip under the scope at 4x to verify there are no cells or debris on the slide. If there are, clean with a kim wipe and DI water. 
- Put the hemocytometer on a kim wipe with the cover slip on. 
- Vortex sample to mix. 
- Using a glass pipette, mix the sample by pipetting up and down, then take a small amount of liquid and load one side of the hemocytometer. 
	- To fill: touch the pipette tip to the inlet groove on the chamber at a 45° angle. Slowly release liquid from the pipette. The chamber should fill via capillary action. If it does not fill completely or forms a bubble, start over. 
- Get a new aliquot of sample and fill the other side of the hemocytometer. 
	- Do not fill both sides from same pipette fill.
- Using a 10x objective (or higher), count cells in one side of the hemocytometer. 
	- Count the number of cells in whole squares
