## Nematostella husbandry

These protocols were primarily developed by Ben Glass and other members of the Barott lab. More info about Nematostella husbandry in previous years can be found on Benchling. 

### Materials 

* Bokel refrigerated incubator
* Plastic tubs (cambros)
* Artificial seawater (ASW)
* 20 L carboy
* Brine shrimp hatcheries
* Brine shrimp eggs (dry, kept at 4°C)
   - Order new from brineshrimpdirect
* Light for hatchery
* Transfer pipettes
* Falcon tubes (15 and 50 mL)
* Cell strainers
* Glass bowls
* Strip light with timer for incubator
* Petri dishes
* Homogenizer

### Relevant background 

This was compiled by Ben Glass. 

Nematostella vectensis is an estuarine, infaunal sea anemone; as such, this species is cultured in the dark and in artificial seawater (ASW) with a much lower salinity and temperature than the open ocean. We culture our lines in ASW at 12 ppt, which we call "1/3-ASW" because it has a salinity ~1/3 of that of the open ocean, and use a Bokel refrigerated incubator to keep the lines at 18°C. 

Anemones are happy to be cultured in large groups and in a variety of containers. In the Barott lab, we use plastic tubs (cambros). In a pinch, the tubs can be put on the bench for days or weeks without harming the population BUT the water will evaporate faster outside of the incubator so be mindful of this.

The care for the anemones can be conceptualized as a 2-week cycle beginning after spawning and proceeding until the following spawning. Between these consecutive spawning cycles, anemones are in gametogenesis (gametogenic cycle is ~2 weeks long), and the only care for them is feeding (with Artemia nauplii) and removing excess waste from the tubs. Anemones will spawn best when on a highly consistent 2-week spawning cycle. Anemones can be spawned in groups within the tubs (this will promote fertilization) or individually in plastic cups (this will allow you to sex anemones if needed but can prevent fertilization as eggs released early will start to decline in health).

As of March 2025 we have one line in the lab, which is called the "AB-1" line (Angela Schmitt and Ben Glass raised this line from gametes in the lab). Relatedness among the anemones in this line ranges from presumably genetically unrelated to full sibling, and the sex ratio is ~50:50 (this species is gonochoric).

### Preparing 1/3 ASW

- We generate seawater using XXX in the large container to the right of the glass tank. To make seawater in this container, do XXXX. 
	- Based on how we make this seawater for the tank, it will be ~35 ppt. 
- Use the YSI probe to take the approximate salinity of the ASW and calulate how much ASW and DI water should be combined to make 1/3 ASW. 
	- Use this equation: reservoir salinity * X L = 12 ppt * desired final volume L
	- For example, say the salinity in the reservoir is 35 ppt and you want to make 20 L of 1/3-ASW (i.e., fully refill the carboy from empty) → 35 ppt * X L = 12 ppt * 20 L, so X = ~6.85 L, and you should thus combine 6.85 L of ASW with 20 L - 6.85 L = 13.15 L of DI water. 
	- There is also a line on the carboy that notes how much ASW to add to male 1/3 ASW.

	- There is also an ASW specific carboy that you can filter water into. 
- Put the specified amount of ASW directly into the 1/3 ASW carboy. Add the specified amout of DI water. 
- Once the ASW and DI water are in the carboy, mix them together with your hand or other tool (or else the ASW just will sit on the bottom and not mix with the DI). 
- Use the YSI probe to measure the salinity again and make adjustments as needed by adding either more ASW or DI water. 
	- **The acceptable salinity range for our anemones is 12-13 ppt.** 

### Hatching brine shrimp for feeding

The dish-style hatchery operates on the positive phototaxis of newly hatched brine shrimp to automatically separate them from their shells without requiring aeration. The brine shrimp eggs are placed in the darkened outer ring of the dish, where empty shells and unhatched eggs remain trapped after hatching. Over 24 to 48 hours, the free-swimming brine shrimp instinctively navigate through the internal concentric labyrinth and under the white ring toward the ambient light. 

- Starting with a clean and dry hatchery, place the white part into the center with the "tabs" facing down to lock this part into position. 
- Fill the hatchery with ASW from the outer ring so that the water flows inward to the middle circle. Fill until the white part is half submerged. 
	- DO NOT use 1/3 ASW to hatch brine shrimp--they will not hatch in 1/3 ASW. Use full strength ASW. 
- Place the hatchery under the bright light on the shelf to the left of the glass tank. Add dry eggs (~1/2 scoop) to the outer circle.
	- Dry brine shrimp eggs are kept at 4°C on the door of the mini fridge to the left of the sink. 
- Place the top on the hatchery and make sure the tab on the side is "Closed" so that light won't enter. 
	- Try not to move the hatchery around too much, as eggs may get stuck on the side and not hatch. 
- Write the day you started the eggs on the whiteboard on the same shelf (to the right of the hatchery).
- Shrimp will start hatching in 24-36 hrs and continue to hatch for 72 hrs. Their nutritional value goes down over time, so we only use shrimp that have been hatched in the past 24-36 hrs for feeding. 

We have two hatcheries that can be rotated and started at different times to ensure that we always have fresh shrimp for feeding. 

### Feeding and cleaning 

We feed the anemones live brine shrimp 2-3x a week. More feeding typically promotes more spawning, so feed a lot (ie daily) if you need lots of gametes.

- Hatch the brine shrimp in full strength ASW and let them hatch for at least 24 hrs before feeding to anemones. 
- Take anemones out of the incubator and put on the benchtop.
- Before feeding, look in containers for large particulate waste and remove with transfer pipette.
- Use a transfer pipette to collect shrimp from a hatchery into a 50 mL tube. Pour them through a cell strainer to catch and collect the shrimp. 
	- Collect shrimp from the center opening with the pipette and do not move the hatchery out of the light or take the top off while collecting shrimp. 
- Flip the cell strainer over each container. With a transfer pipette, use the water from the containers to wash shrimp equally into each container.
- Return anemones to incubator after feeding.

### Feeding juveniles 

The juveniles and newly budded individuals are slightly smaller so it may be harder for them to get food. 

- Spot feed small individuals directly or move many of the smallest individuals to a new tub to help them get better access to shrimp.
- If there are juveniles being raised up that are too small to eat whole brine shrimp, you can use the homogenizer to prepare a slurry. 
	- Add 5-10 mL of shrimp in 1/3-ASW to a 15 mL tube
	- Homogenize at 25,000 rpm for 15 seconds and pour into container with juveniles
	- This might make the water bit gunky, especially if overdone. You can water change the juveniles as needed by pouring through a modified Ziploc with a mesh bottom, then inverting over a tub and using the squirt bottle with 1/3-ASW to wash the juveniles out into a clean tub (similar to what you might do for the shrimp).

### Spawning and water changes (every two weeks)

Ideally, we spawn our anemones every 2 weeks, regardless if the gametes will be used for experiments. It's best to keep them on a regular schedule. 

**IMPORTANT**: Spawning must be set up **at least 14 hrs before** the desired spawning time. Back calculate your time to decide to ramp for spawning induction. 

- Use the ramp feature on the incubator to set the temperature.
	- Anemones need to be at 24°C under lights for 14 hrs to spawn.
- There is a strip light with a timer in the incubator. Set timer to current time, push down pegs for the time you want the light on, and then plug the lights into the timer. 
- On the day of spawning, remove the anemones from the incubator and place them on bench under ambient lights. 
	- Some will spawn overnight, others will take 3-4 hours after being removed from the incubator to spawn.
- During spawning, you can move egg bundles to Petri dishes for culture using transfer pipette with cut tip. Add sperm as well for fertilization. 
- If you don't need the offspring before 3 days post fertilization (dpf), put them in the other incubator at 25°C. 
	- They will settle by 7-11 dpf
- If you need the offspring before 3 dpf, follow the protocol [here](https://github.com/JillAshey/Ashey_Barott_Lab_Notebook/blob/main/posts/2025-11-06-Nematostella_spawning.md) to dejelly and fertilize the eggs. 
- After spawning, do water changes on all the containers and feed the adults before putting them back in the incubator (incubator should be back to 18°C). 
	- To change water, remove the anemones from their containers using a transfer pipette with the tip removed. Dump out the water and rinse the container. Fill container a quarter with 1/3 ASW and put anemones back in. 
- Unplug the lights in the incubator.

**NOTE**: In spring 2026, we started doing water changes the day before spawning to see if that was a cue for them. This can be incorporated into the protocol if needed.
