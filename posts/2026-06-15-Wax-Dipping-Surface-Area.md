## Wax dipping protocol for coral surface area 

The surface area of the coral skeleton is needed for us to normalize our physiological metrics. We use the wax dipping method to determine coral surface area. Read [Veal et al. 2010](https://aslopubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.4319/lom.2010.8.241) for more on the method. 

### Materials 

- Wax bath (crock pot)
- Drying oven
- Analytical balance 
- Variety of wooden dowels -- these will be used to make a standard curve that we will use to calculate surface area of our fragments. 
- Paraffin wax
- Tweezers 
- Foil 
- Skeletons 
- Thermometer

### Protocol 

- After [airbrushing](https://github.com/JillAshey/Ashey_Barott_Lab_Notebook/blob/main/posts/2025-11-11-KBay-Airbrushing.md), dry the skeleton in the drying oven at 60C for ~24 hrs. 
	- Make sure to keep the waterproof tag under the skeleton so we can keep track of who is who. 
- Heat the wax bath to 55-65C. This may take some messing around with the dial on the front. 
	- Do not dip anything in the wax until it is 55-65C! 
- Label 3-5 wooden dowels with a sample number (ie S1, S2).
- Weigh each dowel and record their weight to the nearest 0.0001g. 
- Using the tweezers, submerg the dowel in the wax bath for 2 seconds. 
- Remove the dowel and rotate it quickly in the air in a circular motion to harden the wax. 
- Place dowel on foil and repeat for the rest of the dowels. 
- Weigh wax-dipped dowels once they have cooled (~5mins) to the nearest 0.0001g. 
- Once the coral skeletons are dry, weigh all skeletons to the nearest 0.0001g.
- For each fragment, use the tweezers to submerge it in the wax bath for 2 seconds, remove it, and rotate it to dry the wax. Set it aside (with its ID tag) and continue with the rest of the skeletons. 
- Once the wax-dipped fragments have cooled, weigh them all to the nearest 0.0001g.
